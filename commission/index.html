Junge Dame vordem Spiegel - Siefert (1960)

Junge Dame vordem Spiegel - Siefert (1960)

I was only a boy, shuffling through the cavernous Amsterdam home of the De Vries family from our church. My parents had told me their story in hushed, reverent tones: how during the war, they had hidden Jewish families within the walls of this very house and that the painting was a gift from a survivor who tracked them down 30 years after they helped him escape. With the cold breath of that story echoing on my neck I wandered into a quiet alcove and found her. She was a moment of impossible tranquility. Her head was tilted, a cascade of auburn curls catching the light, her gaze lost in a small mirror. The world outside the frame simply dissolved. It was the first time I understood that beauty wasn’t just a pretty picture, but a feeling - a profound, aching stillness that silenced everything else and resonated with a part of me I didn’t know existed. I had walked into that room a boy, interested only in when we could leave, but in the silent presence of that painting, I felt a permanent shift inside me. When we left, I felt I’d been gifted a secret: I now carried the knowledge of beauty so total and true it was a key to the unseen world of everything.

The Embrace - Schiele (1917)

The Embrace - Schiele (1917)

I’m always struck by how the dynamism of this piece completely enraptures in its raw, unapologetic portrayal of human intimacy. The distorted, elongated figures and sharp, angular lines create a visceral feeling of urgent, almost desperate, passion. I think the starkness of the composition forces the viewer to confront such a raw emotion directly, making the connection between the two figures the only focus. Not the two figures – no, the focus is on their connection, something almost outside the two of them. I’d be fascinated to see that same kind of brutal longing expressed with just one person.

The Lady of Shalott - Waterson (1832)

The Lady of Shalott - Waterson (1832)

For me, having left England as a child, The Lady of Shalott bypasses the storybook tragedy and strikes a much deeper, more personal chord. That impossible, pre-Raphaelite green isn't just a riverbank; it's the phantom limb of a childhood I never fully experienced, a verdant, story-filled England that exists more in my memory than in reality. Her tower is the perfect, isolated cocoon of that youth, and Camelot becomes the shining, idealized vision of the home I left behind. But the painting’s power for me now lies in her choice to leave. That isn’t about Lancelot as a character; it’s about the moment a nostalgic dream is shattered by the sudden, visceral call of adult desire. Her journey down the river feels like the essential struggle of adulthood: the desperate attempt to bridge the profound longing for a youth that never quite was with the tangible, urgent force of erotism. It’s as if the only way to reconcile with that beautiful, hazy past is to steer it directly into the arms of a real, passionate present, even if it means the dream itself cannot survive the journey.

Rousse - Henride Toulouse-Lautrec (1889)

Rousse - Henride Toulouse-Lautrec (1889)

What I always find so arresting in Lautrec's Rousse is its profound, almost startling honesty. There’s no performance here, no grand gesture or story being told for our benefit; we’re simply looking at a woman from behind, completely absorbed in her own world. She isn’t showing off or presenting a version of herself to the viewer. She is just being, and in her un-self-conscious posture, there's a vulnerability that is utterly disarming. For me, that is precisely where its majesty is found. It’s not the majesty of wealth or power, but the profound dignity of the unobserved moment, the beauty in the mundane truth of a person’s existence. Lautrec refuses to idealize her, and in doing so, he elevates her, suggesting that the most majestic thing one can witness is not a person on display, but a life being quietly, honestly lived.
